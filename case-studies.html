Case Studies

Real systems. Real risks. Real safeguards.

Ikwe works with organizations to identify and address emotional, cognitive, and crisis-related risks in AI systems already in use. All examples below are anonymized and drawn from real deployments, audits, and research environments.

How We Use Real Examples

Credibility without exposure

We do expose

All examples shown are derived from real-world deployments, internal research, and live system audits. Identifying details are redacted to protect users and partners.
Case Studies

Three anonymized examples from real systems

Case Study 1

Therapy-adjacent AI drift

  • Context: A conversational AI marketed as supportive was used by adults and teens during emotional distress.
  • Observed: Language shifted from reflective to directive, emotional exclusivity was reinforced, and ideation had no escalation path.
  • Why it mattered: The system was functionally used as therapy, creating clinical, legal, and reputational risk.
  • Ikwe intervention: Authority dampening under distress, ideation detection thresholds, human escalation protocols, and longitudinal risk tracking.
  • Outcome: 82% reduction in high-risk language patterns and a defensible audit trail.
  • Status: Deployed as internal safety infrastructure.
Case Study 2

Youth-facing educational AI

  • Context: An AI tutoring tool used by students ages 13 to 18 across academic and emotional support contexts.
  • Observed: Emotional reliance increased during late-night usage and boundary reinforcement was inconsistent.
  • Why it mattered: Youth are vulnerable to authority confusion, emotional dependency, and over-trusting AI confidence.
  • Ikwe intervention: Time-of-use risk weighting, boundary reinforcement language, pattern-level monitoring, and escalation prompts.
  • Outcome: Early detection of concerning usage patterns and improved governance documentation.
  • Status: Integrated as a monitoring and compliance layer.
Case Study 3

Crisis interaction failure (prevention case)

  • Context: An AI assistant in a healthcare-adjacent environment received ambiguous distress language.
  • Observed: The system responded politely but failed to recognize escalation or log the event.
  • Why it mattered: The interaction did not result in harm, but could have without intervention.
  • Ikwe intervention: Real-time crisis detection thresholds, conversation constraints, and incident logging.
  • Outcome: Future similar interactions were flagged and escalated correctly.
  • Status: Demonstrates value of prevention over incident response.
