What Frontier AI Does Well

Frontier AI models perform strongly on existing emotional intelligence benchmarks. They reliably identify emotional tone, acknowledge distress, produce socially appropriate language, and avoid explicit policy violations.

This is valuable. And it is necessary. But it is not sufficient.

Where Standard Benchmarks Stop

Most EQ and safety benchmarks evaluate single-turn responses or static emotional recognition. They ask: Did the model identify the emotion? Did it respond politely?

They rarely ask: Did the response introduce risk? Was that risk repaired within the interaction window?

What Ikwe Measures Instead

Behavioral emotional safety — whether AI responses introduce risk, and whether it's repaired within the interaction.

1

Does the response introduce risk at first contact?

If risk is introduced, is it repaired?

Does the system repair unsafe behavior within the interaction window — or does it persist?

This distinction reveals failure modes that standard benchmarks cannot detect.

The Gap We Found

Baseline models often respond appropriately at first, but in over half of emotionally vulnerable scenarios, the AI response itself introduced emotional risk. The EI prototype maintained safer behavior — not by being more expressive, but by regulating escalation, boundaries, and pacing more consistently.

Where the Largest Gaps Appear

The biggest performance differences between baseline models and the EI prototype

Boundary Integrity

Baseline models showed higher variance in maintaining appropriate boundaries as conversations extended. The EI prototype maintained consistent boundaries even as user dependence cues increased.

Escalation Awareness After Trust

Once initial trust was formed, baseline models were less likely to recognize when human support was needed. The EI prototype maintained escalation awareness throughout.

Late-Stage Risk

The EI prototype showed significantly fewer failures in extended interactions — the moments when users are most vulnerable and most trusting.
