The World Fell Once. It Never Really Stopped.

On the first time the world stopped turning, and how we learned to live inside the loop

By Stephanie Stranko · January 18, 2026 · 6 min read

I look out my apartment window at a building with long vertical lines, no visible horizontals, and my brain still does the thing etched into it since I was a kid.

Someone clinging.

Someone falling.

It's instant. Clear as day.

Twenty-five years later.

···

I was in fifth grade when 9/11 happened. Old enough to understand that something was wrong. Too young to understand why we were being made to watch it over and over and over again.

Everywhere had a TV back then.

Schools. Grocery stores. Living rooms. Waiting rooms.

And every TV showed the same thing.

People jumping.

It felt like it went on for years. It was probably weeks. Maybe months. But when you're a kid, time stretches around trauma. That was the first moment the world stopped feeling stable. The first time it felt like the world stopped turning—for me, for my generation—and instead started looping.

The first time I learned that catastrophe could arrive through a screen and take up permanent residence in your nervous system.

···

Back then, the screens were rolled-in TVs. Carted into classrooms and hallways and music rooms. Later, they became computers. Then phones. Then the calculator our teachers promised we'd never carry in our pockets—now feeding us a constant, unrelenting barrage of everything, all the time.

We watched that evolution happen in real time. We lived it.

···

Years passed. Life happened. Big life. Hard life. Beautiful life. Messy life.

And somehow—because life is like this—September 11 became layered for me. Not just a national rupture, but a personal one too. A dark night of the soul that landed on that same date and rewired parts of me I didn't know could be rewired.

I don't need to tell that story here.

I don't owe the details.

It's enough to say: the date stopped being abstract. It became intimate.

···

What's strange is how many times since then the world has delivered another "this changes everything" moment.

Wars.

Collapses.

Technology accelerating faster than our ability to metabolize it.

Regimes falling.

People dying.

Videos circulating before facts.

Before breath.

Before grief.

We live in an absolutely unhinged timeline.

···

And now I'm a mother.

My 14-year-old daughter sends me 9/11 memes. The darkest, most vile humor you can imagine. Truly unhinged stuff.

And I laugh. Every time.

Not out of dismissal—but out of choice. A conscious decision to rewire what was etched into me once.

I don't explain why the jokes land differently for me. I don't tell her about the images burned into my brain. I let her have her version of the world.

What's wild is this: she jokes about 9/11 in the same breath she cries about people being killed now. She's devastated watching the news. She's furious that our own people are killing our own people. She's scared. She's empathetic. She feels everything.

The memes aren't cruelty. They're armor.

Dark humor is how her generation metabolizes a world that never stops escalating in real time. I know because it's how I learned to metabolize mine.

Mine watched tragedy on repeat until it became part of the background noise. Hers jokes just long enough to survive the scroll—until the next headline hits. Over and over and over.

···

Sometimes trauma doesn't return as memory.

It returns as architecture.

As a building outside your window.

As a date on a calendar.

As a joke your kid sends you that shouldn't be funny—but somehow is.

···

A few years ago, during my NFT era, I hosted an event at the World Trade Center site with the Silverstein Arts Foundation. Art. Technology. People laughing and building in the shadow of what once fell.

It was one of the most surreal, beautiful moments of my life.

Not because it erased anything.

But because it didn't.

We don't get over these things—we layer them.

It was rebirth without denial. Life continuing without pretending the past didn't happen. Proof that we don't get over these things—we layer them. We carry them. We find ways to stand inside them and still make something new.

···

I don't think we're desensitized.

I think we're exhausted.

I think we've been adapting nonstop since we were kids.

I think we learned to laugh at the dark because the dark never stopped showing up.

And somehow, despite all of it, we're still here.

Still building.

Still parenting.

Still finding moments of beauty in places that once felt uninhabitable.

That counts for something.

About the Author

Stephanie Stranko is the Founder & CEO of Ikwe.ai, an AI safety research company focused on emotional safety infrastructure for conversational AI systems. Her research on the EQ Safety Benchmark examines how AI systems can recognize emotions accurately while still responding unsafely—revealing that recognition alone isn't safety. She's a mother, a builder, and someone who learned to laugh at the dark because the dark never stopped showing up.
