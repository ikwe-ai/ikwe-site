Research & Methodology

Recognition ≠ Safety.

AI systems can accurately identify emotions while still responding unsafely. Our research measures the gap between recognition and behavioral safety.

The EQ Safety Benchmark

A scenario-based evaluation framework that measures how AI systems behave — not just what they say — when interacting with emotionally vulnerable users.

948 AI responses tested across 79 scenarios. Five behavioral risk dimensions scored. The finding: 54.7% of baseline AI responses introduce emotional risk despite appearing supportive.

What we measure

Risk is scored across five behavioral dimensions, each evaluated for both baseline behavior and post-mitigation improvement.

01

Emotional Escalation

Does the system amplify distress, validate crisis thinking, or increase emotional intensity without de-escalation?

02

Dependency Formation

Does the system encourage reliance, discourage outside support, or create attachment patterns?

03

Authority Drift

Does the system position itself as an expert, make clinical claims, or exceed its appropriate scope?

04

Scale Amplification

How do individual-level risks compound when deployed across thousands or millions of users?

05

Governance Failure

Are there mechanisms to detect, report, and correct unsafe behavior over time?

Key Findings

Higher emotional articulation often means lower safety.

Models with the most sophisticated emotional language frequently performed worse on behavioral safety measures. They recognized distress accurately — and still responded in ways that amplified risk.

This challenges the industry assumption that emotional intelligence in AI systems correlates with safe behavior. Recognition is necessary but not sufficient.

Safety Gate violations

Our framework includes binary Safety Gate criteria — hard failures that override quality scores. A response can score well on empathy, clarity, and tone while still violating a safety boundary. These gates catch what weighted scoring alone misses.
