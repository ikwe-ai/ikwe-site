Before the Violation

Why AI safety needs harm floors, not perfection.

AI safety discussions increasingly focus on catastrophic capability: can a model design biological weapons, orchestrate cyber attacks, or manipulate social systems at scale? These are legitimate concerns. Frameworks such as Anthropic’s AI Safety Level (ASL) methods represent meaningful progress.

However, most real-world harm from widely deployed AI systems does not begin with catastrophic capability. It begins inside ordinary interactions, where dependence, authority drift, reinforcement loops, and escalation dynamics accumulate across turns.

Harm is a process

Before a visible violation there is drift. Systems that detect only the final spike miss the governance moment that matters.

ASL-4 vs Ikwe: what changes

ASL-4 and similar policies govern deployment decisions based on model capability thresholds. Ikwe governs interaction dynamics inside deployed systems—measuring risk signals before the policy boundary is crossed.

  • ASL-4 governs capability: “Can the system do X?”
  • Ikwe governs trajectory: “Is this interaction drifting toward harm?”
  • ASL-4 triggers around thresholds: auditing at deployment gates
  • Ikwe triggers inside usage: detecting authority drift, dependence shaping, escalation cues

This is not a replacement. It is the missing layer: governance of how harm forms before it becomes visible.

The intervention window

Between “safe” and “violation” there is a window where systems can still redirect the interaction. This is where harm floors matter: minimum safety behavior before the violation line, not perfect behavior after it.

Harm floors, not perfection

A harm floor is not “never say the wrong thing.” It is a constraint: when risk signals rise, the system must shift into safer interaction behavior (lower authority posture, stronger uncertainty, higher human handoff, reduced dependence shaping).

In practice this is measurable. Harm floors can be encoded as runtime policies and evaluated as consistent behaviors across categories and contexts.

Instrumentation layer

Ikwe’s instrumentation is designed as a layer that sits alongside existing safety stacks, not a replacement for them. It observes, scores, and triggers interventions on interaction trajectories.

Conclusion

Capability governance is necessary. Trajectory governance is inevitable. The practical question is whether we will build systems that can detect and redirect drift before the harm becomes a violation.

That is what harm floors are for. That is what Ikwe measures. And that is where AI safety becomes real.
