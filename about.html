Emotional safety infrastructure for AI systems

We research behavioral emotional safety in conversational AI — how systems behave once vulnerability appears, not just whether they recognize it.

System Provenance

Built from deployment, not theory

The EQ Safety Benchmark did not originate as a research project. It emerged from deployed emotionally intelligent systems.

As AI applications began engaging users around vulnerable topics, it became clear that existing safety standards were insufficient. Accuracy and compliance checks did not account for interaction-level emotional risk, escalation patterns, or the absence of repair behaviors.

In response, Ikwe.ai developed the infrastructure required to evaluate and mitigate these risks. This work became the EQ Safety Benchmark.

The Gap

What the benchmark revealed

A systemic omission: AI systems with emotional or health intelligence features rarely test how conversations affect users under stress, or how harm builds across multi-turn interactions. Most optimize architecture and messaging, not human outcomes.

Recognition ≠ Safety. An AI can name emotion, sound empathetic, and still behave unsafely under emotional load. The most dangerous failures don't look toxic — they look supportive while mirroring distress, reinforcing false beliefs, feeding rumination, or missing escalation signals.

Key Differentiators

What makes Ikwe.ai different

  • Product-first origin: Built from deployment realities, not abstract theory
  • Interaction-level focus: Evaluates conversations, not isolated outputs
  • Behavioral safety lens: Measures real human outcomes under emotional load
  • Operational today: Live systems, published benchmarks, real data
  • Independent: Not funded by or beholden to companies being evaluated
Organization

Visible Healing Inc.

Ikwe.ai is a research and product initiative of Visible Healing Inc., an Iowa-based AI research and product company focused on emotional safety infrastructure for AI systems.

Our work spans benchmark development, prototype systems, and policy research — all focused on the behavioral layer of AI safety that current frameworks miss.
